Why this list

  • 01 Most auto transport bookings in the United States flow through a small number of large, well-funded national brokers. Those companies have scale and slick websites. They also have call-center sales teams compensated on quote volume, dispatch operations several layers removed from the customer, and customer-service patterns that show it.
  • 02 Independent brokers are the alternative. The seven companies on this list are privately held, operate without outside investor pressure to grow at any cost, and answer to customers rather than to growth metrics. Tenure on this list ranges from 5 to 30 years. Carrier networks range from regional to nationwide. What unifies them: operational accountability that does not get diluted by scale.
  • 03 These seven were selected from a longer list using the same six criteria we apply on our main Top 8 list, with additional weight on third-party review consistency and long-term complaint resolution patterns. Pay-to-list inclusions are excluded. The rankings are updated quarterly.

FAQ

Common questions.

01

What makes an auto transport broker “independent”?

We define independent as privately held, not venture-capital backed, and not part of a larger logistics holding company. Most of the companies on this page have been operating for over a decade under continuous ownership. Independence in this industry can mean smaller call-center operations and route coverage, but often more consistent customer service.

02

Are independent brokers more expensive than national brokers?

The short answer is it depends, but usually the difference is small. Independent brokers often quote slightly higher headline rates because they are less aggressive on the load board than larger competitors trying to win bookings at any cost. The trade-off: the quote you receive may be more likely to be the final price for your move.

03

How do I know an independent broker can handle my route?

Carrier network size matters less than carrier network depth on the specific route you need. A broker with 2,000 carriers concentrated on the routes you care about will dispatch more reliably than one with 10,000 carriers scattered across states and cities you don’t need to ship to. When evaluating an independent operator, ask directly: “How many shipments do you dispatch monthly on my origin-to-destination lane?” Brokers that answer with a specific number have the data; brokers that hedge are estimating.
